02 — Deep Dive

How I Think About Healthcare AI

Questions recruiters and collaborators usually ask, answered honestly.

I start with the clinical workflow, not the dataset.

Before touching data, I ask: where does this decision actually get made, who makes it, and what happens when the model is wrong? In healthcare, a false negative in a cancer screening model is not a metric. It is a missed diagnosis. That changes everything about how I frame the problem, choose my evaluation criteria, and design the output.

My medical training means I can read the source data the way a clinician would. I know what an ICD-10 code actually represents in practice, what a pathology report is telling you beyond the label, and where EMR data is systematically unreliable. That context shapes every modeling decision I make, and it's context most ML engineers don't have.

I treat fairness as a clinical safety requirement, not a compliance checkbox.

Biased clinical AI doesn't produce lower accuracy scores. It produces worse care for specific populations, often the ones already underserved. Pulse oximeters that overestimate SpO2 in darker skin tones. Sepsis prediction models trained on populations that don't reflect the patients they're deployed on. These are not edge cases, they are systematic failures.

In practice, I evaluate demographic parity and calibration across subgroups as a mandatory step, not an afterthought. I also think carefully about what the training data is actually measuring. Because in healthcare, the data itself often encodes historical disparities. Correcting a model's outputs without addressing its inputs is not fairness work.

Production-ready means a clinician can trust it, audit it, and override it.

Technically, I design for: containerized inference with FastAPI and Docker, CI/CD for model updates, drift monitoring, and rollback capability. But in healthcare specifically, production-ready also means the system has an explainability layer like SHAP, LIME, or counterfactual explanations that lets a clinician understand why the model flagged a patient before they act on it.

A black-box output in a clinical setting is not just unhelpful. It is unsafe. The clinician needs to be able to say "the model recommended this because of X, and I disagree because of Y." That override loop is not a failure of the AI — it is how responsible clinical decision support is supposed to work.

Because clinical care is not a single prediction. It is a sequence of decisions that depend on each other.

A patient presenting with symptoms needs risk stratification, then facility routing, then a referral plan, then compliance support. These are four distinct tasks with four distinct data requirements. A single model cannot do all of them well, but an agentic system can. Because each agent specialises, and the outputs of one inform the inputs of the next.

This is the architecture I built in OncoSight-SSA: four coordinated agents handling cancer screening, facility mapping, referral recommendation, and compliance prediction in sequence. The clinical pathway becomes the pipeline.

Low-resource settings require more careful engineering, not simpler models.

In Sub-Saharan Africa, the context I work in, you often face sparse clinical records, inconsistent documentation, missing demographic variables, and infrastructure that can't support heavy inference pipelines. You cannot just take a model trained on Western EHR data and deploy it. The data environment, disease burden, and failure modes are fundamentally different.

My approach: start with the data you actually have, not the data you wish you had. Design explicit missing data strategies. Use demographically appropriate training sets. Build for the endpoint device before you build the model.

I translate in both directions and that is genuinely rare.

With clinical teams, I avoid metrics that require statistical training to interpret. I don't say "the model achieved an AUC of 0.87." I say "for every 10 high-risk patients the model flags, 7 or 8 actually need intervention and it catches 9 out of 10 patients who would otherwise be missed."

With technical teams, I bring the clinical context that keeps models grounded. I've presented dashboards to a Head of Department and shipped pipelines to production. The ability to operate in both rooms is something I've built deliberately because healthcare AI that clinicians don't trust doesn't get used, and unused AI helps nobody.

Because the combination I bring doesn't get stronger by waiting.

I am currently embedded in clinical training, seeing patients, reading investigations, working in departments. That clinical immersion is live, not recalled. At the same time, I am shipping production AI systems: fullstack agentic applications, RAG pipelines, explainable ML models.

Most medical students aren't building these things. Most ML engineers aren't embedded in clinical rotations. The intersection is where I work, and that perspective is not something you add after graduation. It is something you build by doing both at the same time, which is exactly what I am doing now.
